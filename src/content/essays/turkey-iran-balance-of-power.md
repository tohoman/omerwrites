From a Neorealist balance-of-power perspective, Turkey tries to keep Iran from becoming a regional hegemon. The regional competition between Ankara and Tehran is not merely a product of opportunistic maneuvers but a structural imperative; Turkey's strategic preference for a weakened Iran reflects a desire to consolidate its own regional influence while avoiding both an overpowering Iran and a destabilizing collapse in Iran's state capacity. The Middle East has recently been the scene of tensions that cannot be underestimated, driven particularly by direct clashes between Iran and Israel and ongoing issues between the United States and Iran. The profound transformation following the collapse of the Bashar al-Assad regime in Syria in December 2024, and the resulting shift in regional balances, has brought a critical question to the fore: why does Turkey prefer its neighbor Iran to remain a weak state rather than a powerful one? This article argues that the Republic of Turkey prefers the Islamic Republic of Iran to remain a weakened state with diminished regional prestige rather than a strong, dominant power; it posits that Turkey views the curtailing of Tehran's power as essential to securing its own strategic superiority, cross-border security, and regional leadership.

## Syria: Iran Lost Everything, Turkey Took Over

Assad's fall and Turkey's rapid move into Syria reflect Ankara's attempt to convert Iran's sudden loss of strategic depth into a lasting shift in the regional balance of power in its favor. For a long time, Iran sought to bring Syria under its influence and draw it firmly into the "Axis of Resistance" coalition. When Bashar al-Assad was overthrown in December 2024, Iran lost the time and money it had invested for years. Turkey, wasting no time, stepped in and replaced Iran as the dominant power.

Brian Katulis put it bluntly: the fall of the Assad regime marks the end of the greater Iranian project in the Levant for the foreseeable future. He noted that Iran had invested a great amount of energy and resources over the past decade to maintain its military presence in Syria — a presence that enabled the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) to move weapons into Lebanon and the West Bank. The sudden loss of Assad, he argued, upends the core assumptions that have long underpinned Iranian strategy in the Middle East. These assumptions included the belief that Tehran could permanently maintain a contiguous geopolitical corridor stretching from Baghdad to Beirut through Damascus, to project power into the Levant. Iran had built its policy on relying on proxy networks, such as Hezbollah and various Shiite militia groups, to secure regional dominance without engaging in direct conventional warfare. Assad's sudden collapse shattered this assumption, revealing that state-level support is irreplaceable and proving that the strategic depth Iran had cultivated over decades could vanish overnight.

Turkey moved fast. Following Assad's collapse, Ankara immediately secured agreements with the new Syrian transitional government led by Ahmed al-Sharaa — historically known by his nom de guerre "Abu Mohammad al-Julani," a former leader of the al-Qaeda-linked al-Nusra Front who once had a $10 million U.S. bounty on his head. His transformation from a globally designated militant into a suit-wearing statesman underscores the fluidity of the new Syrian politics. A February 2025 report by Special Eurasia documents that Turkey has negotiated a joint defense pact, including the establishment of Turkish military bases at Palmyra and the T4 airbase in Syria's central desert, as well as expanded access to Syrian airspace. President Trump also commented on Ankara's position in Syria; the Foundation for Defense of Democracies reported that he told Erdogan directly that Turkey had taken over Syria through surrogates.

Chatham House, in its paper "The Shape-Shifting Axis of Resistance," noted that Iran's networks, despite significant setbacks in 2024 and 2025, have shown historical resilience — the story is not over for Iran. But the balance has clearly shifted. Typically, a rapid military expansion of Turkish forces and defense pacts in the Levant might trigger strategic friction with Western allies. However, for both U.S. and Israeli strategic interests, a Syria stabilized under Turkish influence — even through complex local actors — represents a net positive, as it effectively seals the collapse of Iran's land corridor to the Mediterranean.

## The South Caucasus: Turkey Grows, Iran Shrinks

Turkey and Iran have historically been locked in a competition for influence in the Caucasus, especially over Azerbaijan and Armenia. Turkey's support for Azerbaijan and Iran's strategic anxiety over the Zangezur Corridor illustrate competitive balancing behavior: each side adjusts its alliances and corridors to prevent the other from consolidating regional dominance.

The Gulf International Forum described this as an age-old rivalry that strategists in the Ottoman and Safavid Empires would recognize. Its analysis explains how Turkey helped Azerbaijan secure decisive military dominance over Armenia in the Nagorno-Karabakh War of 2020, and again assisted Baku in September 2023 — moves that, the Forum noted, largely came at the expense of Iran, which has been constrained in Central Asia and the Caucasus.

The academic record backs this up. The Journal of Iran and Central Eurasia Studies published a peer-reviewed article by M. J. Javadi Arjmand on Iran and Turkey's rivalry in the South Caucasus, arguing that the post–Cold War power vacuum shaped the pattern of competition between the two states. The 2017 book *The Great Game in West Asia: Iran, Turkey and the South Caucasus* observed that both states have sought to deepen their regional influence, and each has achieved a measure of success in the Caucasus.

More recently, a 2024 article by Golmohammadi and Markedonov examined Iran's growing strategic anxiety over Azerbaijan, noting the sharp expansion of Turkish and Israeli influence in alliance with Baku after 2020. A 2023 CACIANALYST.org analysis goes further, documenting Iran and Russia's disagreements over the Zangezur Corridor and Tehran's mounting concerns over the Turkey–Azerbaijan relationship.

## Iraq: Two Corridors, One Winner

Iran's sanctions-driven weakness in energy transit reinforces a Neorealist pattern in which Turkey uses economic leverage to lock in favorable relative gains. Turkey and Iran are also in direct competition over political influence in Iraq — and the stakes are massive.

Turkey's Development Road project begins at the Persian Gulf and aims to turn Turkey into a bridge between Europe and the Middle East, running a 1,200 km twin rail-and-road corridor from the Grand Faw Port north to the Turkish border. It stands directly opposed to Iran's own land-bridge ambitions, which are built around securing regional hegemony and a "forward defense" line against Israel and the United States — moving the battlefield, via proxy networks, toward the Mediterranean coast and Israel's borders rather than fighting on Iranian soil.

A peer-reviewed article on Dergipark presents the Development Road as a strong alternative to both China's Belt and Road Initiative and the US-led India–Middle East–Europe Economic Corridor, arguing Turkey can become the key Eurasian transit point. Multiple sources confirm Iran sees this as a direct threat: the Arab Center DC named Türkiye, Iran, and Kuwait as the countries most able to disrupt Iraq's plans, and an XCEPT Research paper confirmed Iran is among the states most likely to work against the project. Al-Majalla positioned the Development Road as a direct competitor to the Suez Canal and Iran's land-based routes.

## Energy: Turkey Wants to Be the Hub Iran Cannot Be

Turkey's strategic goal is to become an energy hub connecting Central Asia, the Middle East, and Europe. Sanctions on Iran — combined with Iran's internal crises and corruption — have created exactly the kind of economic weakness that prevents Iran from competing for that role.

A peer-reviewed study in *Heliyon* analyzed Turkey's energy ambitions and its geographic advantage lying between the energy-rich Middle East and energy-consuming Europe, pursuing pipeline transit revenues. CESRAN International's paper on the Turkish gas hub was direct about the competition, explicitly naming Iranian gas as a source Turkey must account for in its hub ambitions.

The numbers tell the same story. The IRAM Center, citing official Turkish Statistical Institute data, shows that Turkey–Iran trade volume dropped to 3.4 billion USD in 2020 — its lowest level in seven years, after the United States re-imposed sanctions following its 2018 JCPOA withdrawal. That was a 42% drop from the 2017 baseline; Turkish exports to Iran fell 18% in a single year. Iran's sanctions-related weakness, in other words, reshapes the trade relationship entirely on Turkey's terms. The Orion Policy Institute confirmed that while Turkey remains a primary exporter of manufactured goods for Iran and a trade route for Iranian goods to Europe, U.S. sanctions have significantly reduced Iran's capacity to act as an independent energy player.

## Turkey's Most Underrated Advantage: Being the Only Broker

By keeping Iran weak yet functional and reliant on Turkish mediation, Ankara engineers a managed imbalance: Iran cannot overturn the four-century power parity, but remains too important to be ignored.

Ankara holds unique contacts inside Iran that no other Western-allied state has. A weakened, sanctioned Iran that cannot build alliances elsewhere makes Turkey irreplaceable as a broker between Tehran and the West. The Atlantic Council documented Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan's direct outreach to Iranian, Egyptian, EU, and U.S. officials during the 2025–2026 Iran crisis, describing Ankara's stated goal as "regional ownership" of security. The Washington Institute for Near East Policy was even more direct, noting that Turkey does not want to see a nuclear Iran next door, since such a development would end the four centuries of power parity between the two states.

That last line captures Turkey's position perfectly. Not an Iran that is destroyed. Not an Iran that goes nuclear. An Iran that stays weak, stays stable, and keeps needing Turkey.

In summary, Turkey's geopolitical strategy regarding Iran seeks neither total collapse nor a regional hegemon armed with nuclear power. For Ankara, the ideal scenario is one where Iran remains weak yet retains an international presence. This fragile balance affords Turkey two significant advantages: first, it allows Turkey to maintain the role of a crucial bridge between Tehran and the West; second, it protects the long-standing balance of power that has existed for four centuries. Ultimately, by transforming its neighbor's weakness and isolation into direct geopolitical leverage, Turkey strengthens its own regional influence.
